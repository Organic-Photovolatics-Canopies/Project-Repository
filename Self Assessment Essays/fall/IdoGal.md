Senior Design Project Self-Assessment — Fall

Ido Gal

My senior design project develops AI models to design carbon-based polymers that act as donor and acceptor materials for the active layer of an organic solar cell. From my CS perspective, the technical goal is to target a specific absorption bandgap and peak wavelength that meet application constraints. Our team has molecular structures for more than 200 materials and corresponding absorption curves, giving us a rich, labeled corpus to learn from. I will use Graph Neural Networks to map molecular graphs to absorption spectra and learned descriptors. I will pair that with Generative AI to propose donor and acceptor structures that are likely to realize the desired wavelengths and bandgaps. We may initially focus on donor design because many donors respond in the visible spectrum we aim to transmit, making donor control central to our objectives.

My curriculum supplied the foundations I will draw on throughout this work. In Data Structures and Algorithms, I learned complexity analysis, graph traversals, and memory-efficient preprocessing for large datasets. Database design taught schema design, SQL optimization, and data versioning that I will use to curate OPV corpora and track provenance. CS 4032: Machine Learning and Deep Learning covered model selection, regularization, and gradient-based optimization essential for GNN training. Operating Systems and Software Engineering emphasized reliability, testing, and CI/CD, which I will apply to make our pipeline maintainable.

My co-op at Datirium as a Software Engineer trained me to execute end-to-end with accountability. I built Angular components, integrated Firebase/Firestore, and wrote Bash and JavaScript "satellite" scripts to orchestrate data movement. I maintained Jenkins pipelines, performed a MongoDB-to-Firestore migration, and wrote unit and end-to-end tests to keep regressions rare. Those experiences translate to ML by enforcing disciplined repos, modular data loaders, automated preprocessing, and observable training runs. Non-technically, I practiced stakeholder communication, time boxing, and iterative planning, which will guide teamwork and documentation. I will carry forward code reviews, feature branching, and test coverage targets so experiments are reproducible and failures are debuggable.

I'm excited about this project because it will strengthen my AI knowledge beyond my current foundation in software engineering. This effort lets me push into modern machine learning while still leveraging my strengths in testing, data tooling, and reliable software practices. I'm especially motivated by real-world impact: OPV advances can support clean energy and also benefit agriculture by powering distributed sensors, greenhouse coverings, and low-cost monitoring devices. Working at the interface of computer science and materials challenges me to translate domain insights into dependable tools my teammates can actually use. I'm committed to transparency through clear documentation, versioned datasets, and model cards so results are trustworthy and reusable. Ultimately, I want to grow as an AI engineer while contributing something that matters beyond the classroom.

My approach starts with learning the landscape: studying prior OPV models, surveying datasets and features, and extracting best practices worth replicating. I will assemble the corresponding data needed for model creation, standardize molecular and device metadata, and build a clean, reproducible featurization and training pipeline. After reproducing strong baselines from the literature, I will design a GNN tailored to molecular graphs and, where useful, integrate an LLM to mine structured features from papers. To test generalization, I will use scaffold and temporal splits, k-fold cross-validation, and ablation studies on descriptors, architectures, and pretraining tasks. Expected results include a reproducible repository, data cards, baseline-parity reproductions, and a new model that improves error over baselines on OPV benchmarks. I will self-evaluate against predefined metrics and engineering goals.
